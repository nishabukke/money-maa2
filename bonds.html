Corporate Bonds

A corporate bond is a debt security issued by a corporation to raise capital. When an investor purchases a corporate bond, they are essentially lending money to the issuing company in exchange for periodic interest payments and the return of the principal amount at maturity. Corporate bonds are a way for companies to secure funding for various purposes, such as expansion, research and development, or debt refinancing.

Key Features

  • Issuer

    • The issuer of a corporate bond is a corporation seeking to raise capital. These issuers can range from large, well-established companies to smaller firms.

  • Coupon Rate

    • The coupon rate is the fixed interest rate that the issuer agrees to pay to bondholders periodically. This rate is usually set when the bond is issued and remains constant throughout the bond's term.

  • Credit Rating

    • Corporate bonds are assigned credit ratings by rating agencies such as Moody's, Standard & Poor's, and Fitch. These ratings assess the creditworthiness of the issuer and provide an indication of the bond's risk level.

  • Maturity Date

    • The maturity date is the date when the principal amount of the bond becomes due and is repaid to the bondholder. Corporate bonds can have varying maturity periods, ranging from a few years to several decades.

  • Face Value

    • The face value, also known as the par value, is the nominal value of the bond. It represents the amount the issuer will repay to the bondholder at maturity. Corporate bonds are typically issued with a face value of $1,000.
